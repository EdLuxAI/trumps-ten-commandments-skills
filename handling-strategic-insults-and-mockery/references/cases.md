# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 9 unless noted.

## The sticky nickname catalog (operator 1)

| Target | Nickname | Captured perceived weakness | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hillary Clinton | "Crooked Hillary" | Ethics/trust concerns (emails) | Stuck; dominated 2016 coverage |
| Joe Biden | "Sleepy Joe" | Age/energy concerns | Stuck through 2020 and 2024 |
| Marco Rubio | "Lil Marco" | Youth/stature | Stuck in 2016 primary |
| Ted Cruz | "Lyin' Ted" | Trust on specific claims | Stuck in 2016 primary |
| Jeb Bush | "Low-Energy Jeb" | Campaign energy perception | Devastating in 2016 primary |
| Bernie Sanders | "Crazy Bernie" | Democratic-socialist framing | Stuck moderately |
| Elizabeth Warren | "Pocahontas" | Native American ancestry claim | Stuck; weaponized |

**Decision effect:** every successful nickname captured something audiences already partly
recognized. The nickname didn't invent the weakness; it named and memorialized it. For a
defender, this means: the underlying perception must be addressed before the label can be
killed. Attacking the label alone reinforces it via negation.

## The failed-nickname catalog (operator 1, boundary)

| Target | Attempted nickname | Why it failed |
|---|---|---|
| Kamala Harris | "Laffin' Kamala" | Her laugh was not a caricaturable weakness for most audiences |
| Kamala Harris | "Comrade Kamala" | "Socialist" framing didn't map to widely-recognized policy positions |
| Barack Obama | "Cheatin' Obama" | No consensus-recognizable weakness matched the label |

**Decision effect:** the operator has limits. Engaging a failed-nickname attempt amplifies it.
The rule: by the fourth or fifth public repetition, if you're not hearing the nickname in
neutral coverage, it's not working. Don't help.

## Rand Paul, 2015 GOP debate (operator 2)

Rand Paul challenged Trump's poll numbers from the stage. The poll criticism was not unfounded.

Trump's response: not a defense of the poll numbers, not a counter-challenge to Paul's own
polling. Instead, personal mockery of Paul's looks — "should not even be on stage."

**Decision effect:** operator 2 in its clearest form. Any criticism, however factual, is met not
with counter-argument but with personal attack. The disproportion is the deterrent: marginal
critics watching learn that criticism costs a personal humiliation.

For a would-be critic: decide in advance whether the criticism is worth the return fire. For a
target-under-attack: refuse the personal terrain, return to the factual claim.

## Megyn Kelly exchange, 2015 primary debate (operator 3)

Kelly asked Trump about his track record of disparaging women.

Trump's reply: "Only Rosie O'Donnell."

Uproarious laughter from the audience. The line landed. The GOP primary electorate laughed
along. The laugh gave permission.

**Decision effect:** operator 3 at its most visible. The laughter — not the reply itself — did
the political work. Had the audience not laughed, the line would have been cruel without
payoff. With the laugh, it became entertainment and signal-of-strength.

For a defender: the chosen audience for the joke is the permission-grantor. Deny the laugh by
shifting venue or by third-party commentary ("this isn't funny") — you cannot usually deny the
laugh by your own direct objection, because solemnity feeds the comedy.

## Rosie O'Donnell cycle (operators 3, 4, 6)

Pre-politics: Trump and O'Donnell publicly feuded — Trump calling her names, mocking her weight,
ridiculing her TV career.

Sonnenfeld's reading: this was strategic positioning. Trump knew O'Donnell would be the perfect
foil — her public persona and what she represented to Trump's eventual audience were ideal for
sustained combat, and the tabloids ran with the feud.

In the 2015 Megyn Kelly exchange, Trump deployed "Only Rosie O'Donnell" as a pre-built callback
to a multi-year setup.

**Decision effect:** the operator is not "pick a target and insult them once." It's "cultivate a
foil over years so that a single-word reference to them serves as shorthand for a whole
political position." For a defender, this is why responding to a specific insult often matters
less than noticing the pattern of *who has been pre-positioned* as future foils.

## Mexican "rapists and drug dealers" → salsa dancing (operator 6 + boundary of operator 1)

2015 campaign launch: Trump called Mexican immigrants "rapists" and "drug dealers." The rhetoric
helped rally the 2016 primary base.

2024 campaign: Trump needed Hispanic votes to win Electoral-College swing states. He released
Spanish-language ads, danced salsa at events, and softened the rhetoric materially.

**Decision effect:** operator 6 (unpredictability/shape-shifting). Strategic insulters are not
committed to any specific insult — they are committed to whichever insult serves the current
need. A 2024 critique of Trump for his 2015 Mexican rhetoric hit a different audience than
existed in 2015 and could be deflected with contemporary salsa-dancing imagery.

For a defender: if the insulter's positioning shifts, the defender's counter must track the
current position, not the archival one. The 2015 rhetoric is not the 2024 rhetoric.

## Marvin Roffman, Wall Street analyst, ~1988 (operator 2, in skill #9 context)

This case is more fully documented in skill #9 (`predicting-who-the-leader-keeps-drops-and-attacks`)
but the `disproportionate-counterpunch` element belongs here.

Analyst Marvin Roffman published (correctly, as it turned out) that the Trump Taj Mahal would be
unable to generate enough revenue to cover debts. Trump faxed the chairman of Roffman's firm
(Janney Montgomery) demanding Roffman be fired and a public apology issued, threatening suit.

The firm folded, terminated Roffman.

**Decision effect:** the counterpunch was not a response to Roffman personally — it was to
Roffman's firm. The asymmetric power (Trump's potential business to the firm vastly exceeded
Roffman's revenue to the firm) made Roffman disposable. This is the "calibrated-to-prominence"
signal in operator 2: the attack goes where the target is weakest, which is usually not at the
target directly but at whoever holds leverage over them.

For a defender whose criticism exposes them to retaliation via their employer/publisher/etc.:
build direct audience reach (owned media) so that your employer is not the pressure point.

## Humor as self-effacement — the exception (operator 3 boundary)

Ch. 5 material: in private settings Trump is capable of self-effacing humor. A late-1990s
computer-company commercial where Trump parodied himself — bungling a pottery wheel, nickeling
and diming passersby, hitting on a pottery artist who rolled her eyes — is cited as evidence.

**Decision effect:** not all of the insulter's humor is strategic. When the humor is at their
own expense, the right counter may be to laugh along rather than treat it as attack. The
distinction matters because misreading self-effacement as strategic insult makes the defender
look humorless and rigid.

Detection rule: is the laugh at the insulter's expense or at someone else's? Self-effacement
laughs: let them. Attack laughs: deny permission.

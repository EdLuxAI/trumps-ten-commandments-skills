# Rejected Candidates

Material considered for inclusion in this skill and cut, with the reason. Required per the
distill-to-skill framework as visible evidence of the discipline applied.

Most cuts fall into two categories:
1. **Color / illustration** — vivid but not decision-shaping.
2. **Belongs in another skill in this corpus** — the material is real but lives more naturally in
   one of the other seven skills.

## Cut as color (belongs in cases.md if anywhere)

### "Dear leader" cabinet meetings
The Ch. 1 set-piece where cabinet secretaries publicly praise Trump in turn. Vivid illustration, but
the decision-shaping content is already covered by `hub-and-spokes-detection` (lieutenants lacking
independent credentials) and `loyalty-is-one-way-and-vengeance-is-selective`. Parking the episode in
`cases.md` under the hub-and-spokes operator is enough.

### Look-alike young female staffers
Ch. 1 has a detailed passage on Trump's preference for a particular appearance among female
staffers, the "Mad-Dual Hatter" allusion, comments on Melania's hair and Kristi Noem's cosmetic
transition. Omitted from runtime operators. Reason: even if empirically accurate, the pattern does
not change what a user working under a centralizing leader should *do* — the decision effect is
zero. If preserved at all it belongs in source-notes as Sonnenfeld's commentary, not as an operator.

### The golf curation / connector-of-people details
Ch. 5 has substantial material on Trump curating golf partners (Tiger Woods, Rory McIlroy, etc.) and
bringing CEOs together. Color only; no runtime decision is changed by knowing this.

### Martha Stewart / CBS / Pan Am parallels
*The Hero's Farewell* historical exemplars. They are backing material for why hub-and-spokes is a
real type, but at runtime a user dealing with a centralizing boss does not need a list of 1960s CEOs.
Kept in `source-notes.md` as academic lineage, cut from SKILL.md.

## Cut because they belong in other skills

### Rotating foils / need for enemies
The pattern where a centralizing leader requires a constant stream of adversaries to rally their
base. Belongs in skill #6 (`staying-focused-when-they-flood-the-zone`) and skill #5
(`fighting-a-lie-that-gets-repeated-into-truth`). A user inside a centralizing org does not need
this operator to make decisions about their own position.

### Fluidity of friends-foils-foes
The "art of the flip" where enemies become allies become enemies again. Belongs in skill #8
(`reading-winner-sort-and-retribution-calculus`) because the decision effect — predicting whether
someone will be rehabilitated — is the core of that skill. Here it would duplicate operator 6.

### Divide-and-conquer across external institutions
Ch. 3 has extensive material on attacking law firms, universities, business coalitions. This is
skill #4 (`standing-together-against-divide-and-conquer`). Only the internal-to-the-org version
— pitting lieutenants against each other — belongs in this skill.

### Grandeur and self-image operators
All of Ch. 10 and parts of Ch. 1 deal with "I alone can fix it," inflated numbers, gold-everything.
Real operators, but they belong in skill #8 (`seeing-through-a-leaders-grandiose-armor`). Including
them here would conflate two distinct diagnostic frames: how the *org* is shaped vs. how the
*leader* presents themselves. A user may need one and not the other.

## Cut because too politically loaded to generalize

### Specific identifications of people as "losers" or "nobodies"
Sonnenfeld's descriptions of specific ex-staffers (Corey Lewandowski, Reince Priebus, etc.) as
"pathetically loyal" or without non-Trump professional networks. Whether empirically true or not,
re-stating these in the skill risks the skill inheriting the source's editorial voice. The
generalizable claim — that centralizers prefer lieutenants without independent power bases — is
captured in `hub-and-spokes-detection`. The specific identifications are cut.

### Directly partisan framing of the second-term ethics claims
Ch. 4 has detailed partisan framing of specific second-term transactions (Qatar 747, crypto
ventures, Executive Branch Club). The pattern generalizes to
`mix-personal-and-official-interest-detection`, which we folded into
`hub-and-spokes-detection` as cash-flow detection signals. The specific allegations are cut — they
would date rapidly and carry partisan framing into a skill meant to work across political contexts.

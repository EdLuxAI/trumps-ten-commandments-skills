# Source Notes

Where each operator in this skill comes from, and the caveats a reader should hold in mind when
trusting the material.

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld, Jeffrey, and Steven Tian. *Trump's Ten Commandments: Strategic Lessons from the Trump
Leadership Toolbox*. 2025.**

Primarily Chapter 1 ("Trump's Hub-and-Spokes Model of Leadership"), with material from Chapters 3
("Divide and Conquer"), 4 ("How Trump Makes Money" — the ethics-diagnostic cash-flow signals), 5
("Trump Behind Closed Doors"), and 7 ("Trump's World of Winners and Losers").

## Source caveats

**`is_primary_source: false`** — Sonnenfeld is an interpretive observer of Donald Trump, not Trump
himself, and the book is partisan in tone. Sonnenfeld discloses a personal relationship with Trump
spanning 25 years, including periods of friendship, advisory engagement, and public opposition. He
also discloses that he catalyzed CEO opposition to Trump in 2017 (post-Charlottesville) and 2020
(post-election).

The operators in this skill have been abstracted from the person-specific narrative to generic
patterns of **centralizing leadership**. When re-reading the source, hold these biases in mind:

- Sonnenfeld treats some outcomes as normatively bad (institutional erosion, norm-breaking). These
  are marked as `anti_pattern` in our inventory, which is a decision label, not a moral one.
- Cases are drawn overwhelmingly from 2016–2025. Operators generalize across time, but specific cases
  may age poorly.
- The author's own prior work (*The Hero's Farewell*, 1988) is cited as backing for the hub-and-spokes
  typology. This is legitimate self-citation but not independent corroboration.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `hub-and-spokes-detection`

- **Alfred D. Chandler Jr.**, *Strategy and Structure: Chapters in the History of the Industrial
  Enterprise* (1962). Chandler documented early-stage business forms in which the founder-entrepreneur
  sits at the center of a spoke-like structure before the firm scales into multi-divisional form.
  Sonnenfeld invokes Chandler to argue that hub-and-spokes is a natural stage of business evolution,
  not inherently pathological — but becomes pathological when a leader refuses to institutionalize.
- **Larry E. Greiner**, *"Evolution and Revolution as Organizations Grow"*, Harvard Business Review
  (1972). Greiner's five-phase model of organizational growth identifies the "creativity" phase
  (direct founder control) and the "crisis of leadership" that triggers the transition to more formal
  structures. Centralizers who refuse the transition get stuck.
- **Sonnenfeld's own *The Hero's Farewell: What Happens When CEOs Retire*** (Oxford University Press,
  1988). Introduces the "Monarch" and "General" CEO types — leaders who refuse to leave office and
  who cultivate intramural rivalries to remain irreplaceable. Historical exemplars cited: William
  Paley (CBS), Juan Trippe (Pan Am), Harold Geneen (ITT), Robert Woodruff (Coca-Cola), Charles de
  Gaulle (France), Tito (Yugoslavia), Haile Selassie (Ethiopia).

### `acting-vs-confirmed-leverage`

- No deep academic lineage in the book. Sonnenfeld presents it as direct observation. The underlying
  principle is a simple agency-theory one: a principal retains leverage over an agent whose tenure
  depends on the principal's discretion; that leverage drops sharply once the agent is ratified by an
  independent body (Senate confirmation, board vote, shareholder vote).

### `plausible-deniability-via-vague-suggestion`

- **Henry II of England and the Thomas Becket assassination (1170).** "Will no one rid me of this
  meddlesome priest?" — spoken by Henry, acted on by four knights, denied by Henry afterward.
  Sonnenfeld uses this as the archetypal case.
- **Walter Bagehot**, *The English Constitution* (1867). "No man can argue on his knees" — Bagehot's
  observation that subservient posture fortifies conformity among courtiers. Sonnenfeld cites this to
  explain why centralized courts drift toward action-on-inferred-intent.

### `elimination-tournament-selection`

- No formal academic lineage. Sonnenfeld explicitly frames this as a reality-TV importation (*The
  Apprentice* format applied to cabinet selection, VP selection, prosecutorial appointments).

### `reach-before-public-commitment`

- No formal lineage. Derived from Sonnenfeld's direct advisory experience across decades.
- Related concept in the broader literature: **cognitive commitment** and **public consistency bias**
  (Cialdini, *Influence*, 1984), which predicts that publicly stated positions become harder to
  reverse than privately held ones.

### `loyalty-is-one-way-and-vengeance-is-selective`

- Derived from pattern-observation across the book. No single academic source. The selectivity half
  of the operator (that retaliation targets people without sympathetic constituency) is supported in
  skill #8's `winner-vs-loser-by-constituency` operator.

### `invert-hierarchy`

- Sonnenfeld draws an explicit comparison to **Mao, Lenin, and Stalin** as revolutionary leaders who
  systematically inverted established hierarchies to reconstitute authority around themselves. The
  operator generalizes the pattern without committing to the comparison.

### `no-true-friends-only-followers`

- **Carl von Clausewitz**, *On War* (1832). Cited by Sonnenfeld in Ch. 5 for the distinction between
  friendship and alliance based on mutual strategic interest — the foundation of what later became
  known as **Realpolitik** (Ludwig von Rochau, 1853) and was carried forward by Bismarck, Kissinger,
  Brzezinski, and others.
- Sonnenfeld's claim is that a centralizer's worldview is pure Realpolitik applied to personal
  relationships, not only to state affairs.

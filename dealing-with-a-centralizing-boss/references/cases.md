# Cases

Longer-form examples cited inline in `SKILL.md`. Loaded only when the user wants to see the source
material behind an operator, or when a case is directly relevant to their situation.

All cases drawn from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025) unless otherwise noted.

---

## Mattis → Hegseth (operator: `invert-hierarchy`, `hub-and-spokes-detection`)

Ch. 1, Ch. 3.

General James Mattis, a four-star Marine Corps general and commander across multiple wars, served as
Trump's first-term Secretary of Defense. He had independent stature, a credentialed network, and
intellectual standing that made him publicly un-manageable. He and Trump fell out within two years.

Subsequent secretaries of defense were progressively lower-ranked: Mark Esper (lieutenant colonel),
Chris Miller (colonel), Pete Hegseth (major — who had never led any organization larger than a
non-profit he ran into the ground). Dan "Raizin" Caine became the first chair of the Joint Chiefs of
Staff in US history never to have reached four-star rank.

**Decision effect for a user:** if you are the credentialed senior expected to "steward" a
centralizing leader, your formal rank will not protect you. The leader will route around you via
lower-ranked loyalists. Your leverage is your external network, not your title.

## Mike Pence, January 6 (operator: `loyalty-is-one-way-and-vengeance-is-selective`)

Ch. 1, Ch. 7.

Pence served Trump loyally for four years as vice president. On January 6, 2021, he declined to
block Biden's Electoral College certification. Trump turned on him immediately; the crowd chanted
"Hang Mike Pence" as he was evacuated from the Capitol.

Sonnenfeld's framing (Ch. 7): by the end of Trump's first term, the mainstream-conservative
evangelical imprimatur Pence originally brought had been absorbed. Pence had become an
interchangeable constituency-less official. The single refusal to act was enough to trigger full
retaliation.

**Decision effect for a user:** four years of loyalty is not accumulated credit. Your standing with
a centralizer is re-evaluated at every decision point, based on current utility. Be prepared for
the possibility that a single refusal ends the relationship.

## Rudy Giuliani unpaid bills (operators: `plausible-deniability-via-vague-suggestion`,
`loyalty-is-one-way-and-vengeance-is-selective`)

Ch. 1, Ch. 7.

Giuliani carried out what the book frames as the "dirty hatchet jobs" of the 2020 election-challenge
effort. Afterward Trump allegedly refused to pay Giuliani's legal bills and also declined to pay
money owed to Giuliani personally. A pardon came nearly a year into the second term, after repeated
entreaties from allies.

**Decision effect for a user:** if you are asked (explicitly or via suggestion) to take a
consequential risk on a centralizer's behalf, do not rely on future reciprocity. The pattern is:
action → fallout lands on you → centralizer distances → maybe eventual partial rehabilitation if
you still have utility. Plan accordingly before acting, not afterward.

## Peter Navarro, jail time without executive privilege (operator:
`plausible-deniability-via-vague-suggestion`)

Ch. 1.

Navarro served four months in federal prison for refusing to testify against Trump about January 6.
The court found that Trump never formally invoked executive privilege on Navarro's behalf — which,
had it been invoked, might have kept Navarro out of jail.

**Decision effect for a user:** this is the sharpest case in the book for the counter-move "never
execute on inferred intent without written direction." Inferred loyalty actions produce real
criminal and financial exposure that the principal may decline to cover.

## Anthony Scaramucci, 11 days (operator: `elimination-tournament-selection`,
`hub-and-spokes-detection`)

Ch. 1.

Scaramucci served as White House communications director for 11 days before being fired. Sonnenfeld's
framing: Scaramucci's "brash New York persona shone too brightly." The specific firing cause is less
important than the pattern — a lieutenant whose personal brand begins to compete with the leader's
is removed, regardless of tenure.

**Decision effect for a user:** if you are inside a centralizing org and your own visibility begins
to rise, the safe move is to credit the leader publicly at every opportunity. Visibility without
credit-direction is a threat.

## VP selection tournaments (operator: `elimination-tournament-selection`)

Ch. 1.

In 2016, Mike Pence was kept waiting in a hotel for days while Trump publicly flirted with selecting
Chris Christie. In 2024, JD Vance endured weeks of competition with Rubio and Burgum, complete with
leaked opposition research.

**Decision effect for a user:** if you are offered participation in a visibly drawn-out selection
process, understand that the drawn-out-ness is the point. The eventual winner emerges branded as
dependent. If you have an independent power base and can credibly decline, declining may
paradoxically make you more attractive to be recruited outside the public process.

## Jeff Sessions primary loss (operator: `loyalty-is-one-way-and-vengeance-is-selective`)

Ch. 1.

After Sessions recused himself from the Russia investigation — a procedural act — Trump turned on
him publicly. When Sessions later ran for his old Alabama Senate seat, Trump endorsed Tommy
Tuberville and attacked Sessions with enough force that Sessions finished last in the primary,
losing by double digits.

**Decision effect for a user:** one procedural act of independence, interpreted as disloyalty, can
consume a long political career. If your role requires procedural independence from the leader
(recusal, certification, audit), understand that the leader may treat that independence as
betrayal regardless of its legal necessity.

## Acting-vs-confirmed pattern — general (operator: `acting-vs-confirmed-leverage`)

Ch. 1.

Sonnenfeld's observation: Trump's second-term pattern of appointing "acting" agency heads in lieu of
pursuing Senate confirmation preserves his leverage, because interim appointees must continually
seek his favor to earn the formal appointment. Once confirmed, the leverage drops to firing or
icing out.

**Decision effect for a user evaluating an offer:** the difference between "acting" and "confirmed"
is structural, not cosmetic. Confirmation (or equivalent board-level ratification in a corporate
setting) is what converts you from disposable to protected. If confirmation is consistently
deferred, assume it will never happen.

## Cash-flow signals (operator: `hub-and-spokes-detection`, folded ethics content)

Ch. 4.

Examples Sonnenfeld cites of what he calls the personal monetization of a centralized position:

- Secret Service rent at Trump-owned properties
- Foreign officials booking blocks of rooms at Trump National DC (used or not)
- US military refueling stops diverted through Turnberry Resort
- Qatar 747 "donation" with ownership reverting to Trump's presidential library
- Trump Mobile (a China-manufactured phone painted gold and sold as US-made)
- Trump crypto ventures with foreign buyers paying for access
- Executive Branch Club membership fees ($500,000)
- Shell procurement companies marking up goods resold to the Trump Organization (Fred Trump era)

**Decision effect for a user:** when evaluating whether an organization is truly institutional or
whether it is a centralized personal empire wearing institutional clothing, the cash-flow signals
are diagnostic. If the centralizer's personal wealth is moving through entities that blur the
line between official role and private business, the institutional constraints you might be
relying on are weaker than they appear.

**Caveat:** this case section inherits the source's specific framing, which is politically loaded.
The operator itself — detection of personal monetization of a centralized role — generalizes.

# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 8 unless noted.

Caveat: many of these cases are politically charged. The operators apply regardless of which
side of a given political dispute the user is on; the cases are illustrations of the
*mechanism*.

---

## The 2024 campaign narrative contests (operators 1, 8)

Ch. 8.

Sonnenfeld's claim: through confident repetition, Trump displaced the factual record on multiple
policy domains during the 2024 campaign. Democratic officials largely surrendered the
counter-narrative by declining to celebrate their own record.

**Immigration.** Trump's claim: "we had the safest border in history." Data cited by the book:
annual US-Mexico border encounters under Trump 1.0 averaged 488,164 — *higher* than under Obama
(408,493). Yet popular polling consistently showed voters believing Trump was stronger on
immigration than Biden/Harris.

**Economy/inflation.** Trump's claim: Biden caused runaway inflation. Data cited: inflation spiked
to 9.1% in post-pandemic Biden period, but fell to 2.9% by summer 2024, near historic averages.
Economists attribute much of the spike to COVID supply shocks rather than fiscal policy. Voters
consistently preferred Trump on economic issues.

**Deficit spending.** Trump's claim: Biden had the largest deficit in history. On an equivalent
basis, Trump 1.0 added more debt per year than Biden; Trump 1.0 oversaw the largest single-year
spending deficit since WWII. PPP fraud was under Trump.

**Infrastructure.** Trump's claim: he would redirect unspent dollars to roads, bridges, dams.
Data: the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law and IRA dedicated $700B+ under Biden. Biden announced
$454B in funding for 56,000+ projects (165,000 miles of roads, 9,400 bridges, 450 ports, 300
airport terminals). Voters gave Trump comparable infrastructure credit.

**Jobs.** Trump's claim: most Biden jobs went to illegal immigrants. Data: Fed data showed over
half of employment-level changes went to native-born workers.

**Semiconductors.** Trump's claim: best four years for tech/AI/innovation. Data: the CHIPS Act
was passed under Biden after Trump 1.0 did little to incentivize domestic chip production (which
contributed to pandemic-era shortages).

Sonnenfeld's direct testimony: top Biden-Harris economic advisors (Ron Klain, Jared Bernstein)
told him they were reluctant to celebrate their administration's record *because voters believed
Trump's claims that the economy was faltering*. Yellen and Raimondo were dispatched on "misguided
apology tours." Other officials "wrongly attacked CEOs and companies for price gouging."

Republican-leaning CEOs, per Sonnenfeld, were "more appreciative of the genuine Biden/Harris
economic accomplishments than those Biden/Harris administration officials themselves."

**Decision effect:** operator 8's specific form. If your side's officials are not willing to
visibly, confidently celebrate your own genuine wins, the narrative is forfeit regardless of what
the underlying data says.

## Mt. Rushmore and the Nobel Peace Prize (operator 6)

Ch. 8, Ch. 10.

Early in Trump's first term, public suggestions that he should be on Mt. Rushmore or win a Nobel
Peace Prize were treated as jokes — including by many Trump allies.

By the 2025 second term: active efforts by supporters to nominate Trump for the Nobel Peace Prize
and to etch his face on Mt. Rushmore. When the 2025 Nobel went to María Corina Machado instead,
Trump supporters expressed genuine disappointment.

**Decision effect:** a claim that would have been laughable if made suddenly became a mainstream
aspiration through nothing but sustained repetition over ~8 years. The window to kill a planted
claim is early, while it is still ridiculous.

## Origin-myth defense via litigation (operators 1, 3)

Ch. 8, Ch. 4.

The "self-made billionaire" origin myth was defended through:
- Sustained public repetition by Trump across decades.
- $5B defamation lawsuit against biographer Timothy O'Brien (2004–2009) after O'Brien's book
  reported Trump's actual net worth was closer to $250M than the billions claimed. Dismissed by
  the courts, but only after years of litigation costs and O'Brien's eventual departure from
  the *NYT*. Trump later admitted the suit's purpose: "I spent a couple of bucks on legal fees,
  and they spent a whole lot more. I did it to make his life miserable, which I'm happy about."
- Sealing of Trump's own academic records through staff intimidation of educational officials
  while simultaneously demanding Obama's records.

The 2018 leak by Mary Trump of thousands of pages of family financial documents to the *NYT* was
the definitive documentation of the myth's inaccuracy — but did not displace the impression in
public memory.

**Decision effect:** operator 3. Documentation of truth is necessary but not sufficient. The
leaked documents existed; the sustained confident repetition of the false narrative was stronger
than the document release.

## Nickname successes and failures (operator 2 — both sides)

Ch. 8, Ch. 9.

**Successes:** "Crooked Hillary," "Sleepy Joe," "Lil Marco," "Lyin' Ted," "Low-Energy Jeb,"
"Crazy Bernie," "Pocahontas Warren." All landed because each captured a widely-perceived (even
if unfair) weakness. Once affixed, they dominated coverage.

**Failures:** Kamala Harris attempts ("Laffin' Kamala," "Comrade Kamala") fell flat. Barack
Obama ("Cheatin' Obama") never stuck. The book's implicit diagnosis: no single perceived
weakness was sharp enough to caricature; audiences didn't recognize themselves in the label.

**Decision effect:**
- For operator 2: the preemption rule. If you are someone Trump might target, define yourself
  first with a sharp, memorable label. Letting the attacker brand first leaves you to negate,
  which reinforces the brand.
- For `do-not-use-when`: failed nicknames are evidence that the operator has boundaries. Not
  every branding attempt succeeds; engaging with a failed attempt amplifies it.

## "I witnessed Muslims celebrating in New Jersey after 9/11" (operator 1)

Ch. 8.

Trump repeatedly made this claim during 2015–2016. No evidence exists; the specific factual
claim was thoroughly debunked.

The Hovland-Weiss point: the specific facts may have been debunked, but the emotional
impression — "there was celebration, something suspicious happened, the official narrative is
incomplete" — lingered in audiences.

**Decision effect:** for operator 1, the proof that confident repetition can implant an
*impression* even when the specific factual claim is refuted. This is why the counter has to be
at the impression level, not just the factual level.

## Putin's Ukraine framing adopted and echoed (operator 4)

Ch. 8.

Trump's account of the origins of the Ukraine conflict in many public statements tracked
Putin's framing (Ukraine provoked, NATO expansion was the real cause, etc.). Sonnenfeld's
reading: Trump legitimized the external narrative by repetition, whether knowingly or not.

**Decision effect:** for operator 4. The defender counter ("this specific narrative originated
in [source], which has the following motivation…") is more effective than point-by-point
historical rebuttal of the claims.

## Election denial and the sleeper-effect masterwork (operators 1, 5, 8)

Ch. 8.

Trump lost 60+ court cases and no evidence of significant fraud emerged. Yet repeated confident
assertion convinced 60%+ of GOP voters that the 2020 election was stolen.

Specific allegations — voting machines, ballot harvesting, dead voters — were individually
debunked. The *overall impression of illegitimacy* took root regardless.

Even after Trump's 2024 comeback, he continued to insist he won 2020 despite no reason to (he was
now the sitting president).

**Decision effect:**
- Operator 1: the canonical sleeper-effect case in American political history.
- Operator 5: `asymmetric-evidence-acceptance` perfectly illustrated. Every ruling favoring Trump
  was real; every ruling against was "rigged."
- Operator 8: the extent of the Democratic counter-campaign (or lack thereof) is itself a case
  study in ceded-narrative failure mode.

## 2015 false anti-Islamic town-hall claim (operator 8)

Ch. 8.

A supporter at a New Hampshire 2015 town hall made a false anti-Islamic assertion. Trump did not
correct it. Sonnenfeld (at the time a direct advisor) counseled Trump privately that he had
missed "his McCain moment" — referring to McCain's famous 2008 correction of a bigoted supporter.
Trump's reply: "Hey, it wasn't me who said it."

**Decision effect:** `plausible-deniability` meets projection. The attacker can generate
narrative warfare by letting others make claims they don't explicitly endorse, then disowning
them when challenged. The counter is to demand explicit repudiation, not just non-endorsement.

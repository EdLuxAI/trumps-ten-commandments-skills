# Source Notes

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Chapter 8** ("Rewriting History Through
the Sleeper Effect"), with supplementary material from Chapter 2 (projection) and the Conclusion
(defender rules).

## Source caveats

`is_primary_source: false`. The book's 2024-campaign narrative analyses (immigration, economy,
inflation, debt, jobs, manufacturing, infrastructure) are politically contested — reasonable
people disagree about the weight of specific data points. The operators in this skill do not
depend on the specific campaign claims being adjudicated in favor of one side; they are about
the *mechanism* of repeated confident assertion, which operates regardless of who is deploying
it.

Two specific politically loaded framings to hold lightly:
- The claim that Trump personally "repeatedly told" false things. The Hovland-Weiss mechanism
  operates whether the speaker sincerely believes what they are repeating (many do) or is
  cynically calculating (some are).
- The framing of specific Biden-era economic data as "triumphs" Democrats failed to celebrate.
  Whether those data were triumphs is the thing at issue; the operator's claim is the narrower
  one that surrendering narrative by silence guarantees loss regardless of underlying merit.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `relentless-repetition-beats-accuracy`

- **Carl Hovland and Walter Weiss**, "The Influence of Source Credibility on Communication
  Effectiveness," *Public Opinion Quarterly* (1951). The foundational sleeper-effect study. Yale
  Communications and Attitude Change Program, part of the post-WW2 US effort to understand mass
  persuasion (the program was funded partly by concerns about Soviet propaganda and rising
  demagogues).
- **Chris Argyris** (Harvard/Yale), personal communication with Sonnenfeld as cited in Ch. 8.
  Argyris's observation on the sophistication split: sophisticated audiences demand both sides,
  mass audiences accept a single confident version.
- **Joseph Goebbels** is invoked by Sonnenfeld as the historical reference point for Hovland's
  own motivation to study the mechanism. The reference is attribution, not endorsement.

### `define-the-counterparty-before-they-do`

- No single academic source. Connected to **framing theory** in political communication (George
  Lakoff, *Don't Think of an Elephant!*, 2004) — once a frame is active, counter-messages
  evaluated within it reinforce rather than contradict it.
- **P. T. Barnum** (19th c.), "There's no such thing as bad publicity" — attention as currency,
  the precondition for framing.

### `control-flow-of-records`

- **George Orwell**, *1984* (1949). The Ministry of Truth. Party slogan: "Who controls the past
  controls the future. Who controls the present controls the past." Sonnenfeld explicitly
  invokes Orwell.

### `echo-a-useful-external-narrative`

- No formal academic source. Related concept: **laundering** in disinformation studies (RAND,
  Stanford Internet Observatory) — the pipeline from fringe spaces to mainstream discourse via
  amplification by trusted figures.

### `asymmetric-evidence-acceptance`

- Related concept: **motivated reasoning** (Ziva Kunda, *Psychological Bulletin*, 1990) and
  **confirmation bias** (Wason, 1960s). The pattern described here is motivated reasoning deployed
  as public rhetoric rather than as a private cognitive bias.
- **Kellyanne Conway**, "alternative facts" — the term coined on *Meet the Press*, January 22,
  2017. Sonnenfeld notes the term "inelegantly" captured the phenomenon already described by
  Hovland-Weiss 66 years earlier.

### `plant-seeds-and-water-them`

- **Leo Braudy**, *The Frenzy of Renown: Fame and Its History* (1986). Fame is insatiable;
  once on the stage, one cannot step off without vanishing. The book's framework for why
  planted self-aggrandizing claims require constant watering.
- **Orwell**, *1984* again — for the long-timescale manipulation of record.

### `projection-claim-aggrieved-victim`

- **Sigmund Freud**, concept of projection as a defense mechanism (*The Interpretation of
  Dreams*, 1900, and subsequent works). Sonnenfeld uses the term in its popular rhetorical
  sense rather than the strict psychoanalytic one.
- In rhetoric and propaganda studies: **accusation in a mirror** — the technique of accusing the
  opponent of the atrocity one intends to commit or is committing. Documented in Rwandan
  genocide rhetoric (RTLM 1994) and in numerous 20th-century propaganda cases.

### `challenge-false-claims-with-equal-vigor`

- No formal academic source. The specific 2024-campaign diagnosis (Democrats' silence on their
  own economic record as a cause of narrative surrender) is Sonnenfeld's own observation, backed
  by his reporting of direct conversations with Biden-Harris advisors (Ron Klain, Jared
  Bernstein, Janet Yellen, Gina Raimondo).

## Historical parallels cited in the chapter

- **Alan Brinkley**, *Voices of Protest: Huey Long, Father Coughlin & The Great Depression*
  (Pulitzer, 1983) — the American populist-demagogue tradition.
- **Huey Long** (Louisiana, 1928–1935) — populist charm built on factually dubious foundations;
  assassinated before the pattern's full consequences unfolded.
- **Father Charles Coughlin** (radio priest, 1930s) — repeated anti-Semitic and pro-fascist
  narratives to vast audiences.

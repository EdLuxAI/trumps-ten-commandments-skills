# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 4 unless noted.

## The Commodore Hotel / Grand Hyatt, 1976–1980 (operators 1, 2, 3)

Trump's first Manhattan deal. Penn Central Railroad was in bankruptcy; the Commodore Hotel next
to Grand Central was for sale. Trump was 30, unknown, with no capital.

Structure, as engineered:
- Mayor Abe Beame was a longtime friend of Fred Trump. Beame instructed his administration: "I
  support whatever Donald and Fred want."
- The state's Urban Development Corporation **bought the property outright** — Trump did not buy
  it.
- Trump got a **99-year lease**.
- Trump negotiated a **40-year property tax exemption** — unprecedented, the first ever for a
  commercial property in NYC.
- **Fred Trump guaranteed** the $70M construction loan.
- **Hyatt Corporation** (Pritzker family) came in as operating partner.

Cost to NYC: one study found the tax exemption cost the city more than $410M in lost revenue.

Trump's personal capital at risk: effectively zero.

If it had failed, the bag-holder would have been: NYC government (tax abatement), Fred Trump
(loan guarantee), Hyatt (operating losses).

It didn't fail. Trump sold his stake to Hyatt in 1996 for $142M.

**Decision effect:** the template was set on deal one. Distressed seller → political connection
→ public capital → OPM construction financing → operating partner absorbing downside → Trump
captures upside. Every element is a separate operator.

## Atlantic City bankruptcies, 1988–1995 (operators 4, 5)

Trump's casino empire (Taj Mahal, Trump Plaza, Trump Castle) collapsed under ~$4B of debt, ~$1B
personally guaranteed. This is the rare case where the template failed on Trump himself.

The internecine-creditor-warfare pattern:
1. Trump issued $775M of 14% junk bonds to refinance, despite explicit promises to earlier
   creditors that he wouldn't. More than half the proceeds went to pay Trump's personal loans
   rather than into the casinos.
2. Trump took Trump Hotels and Casinos public, pitting new shareholders against existing
   creditors.
3. Trump issued another $155M of 15.5% junk bonds, creating a third class of creditors with yet
   different priorities.

Each creditor class fought the others over who got paid first. They could not coordinate enough
to hold Trump accountable because any one of them that refused concessions risked being the odd
one out.

Outcome: Trump offloaded ~$1B of personal liabilities onto investors and creditors. He still lost
his stakes in the Plaza Hotel, Taj Mahal, Trump Plaza AC, and Trump Castle; liquidated Trump
Shuttle and Trump Princess; was placed on a spending allowance by his bankers.

Fred Trump had to help meet an interest payment by sending a crony to buy $3M of casino chips as
a disguised gift.

**Decision effect:** (1) If you are one of several creditor classes, coordinate with the others
directly — divide-and-conquer only works on uncoordinated targets. (2) Even a master of OPM can
fail by taking on personal guarantees. Personal guarantees are where the upside-only structure
breaks down.

## The Wollman Rink "rescue", mid-1980s (operator 1, plus appearance-vs-substance)

Ch. 4.

NYC had spent millions over half a decade trying to fix Central Park's Wollman ice-skating rink.
Trump, feuding with Mayor Koch, offered to finish the project within six months "at his expense"
in exchange for operating leases on the rink and a restaurant.

Reality:
- The project finished ahead of schedule and under budget — Trump's jawboning generated huge
  public attention.
- But most expenses were **reimbursed by the city**. Trump's actual out-of-pocket outlay was
  minimal.
- Trump promised to donate operating profits to charity. City audits later found Trump had
  **significantly underreported revenues** (cash receipts, concession sales, skating admissions).
  He paid $120,000 back.
- He continued operating the concession until 2021 (severed after January 6).

**Decision effect:** deals framed as philanthropy or civic rescue often contain subsidy and
revenue streams that accrue to the "philanthropist." When evaluating a partnership where the
counterparty frames their participation as generosity, ask: what does their actual cash flow look
like, and what do they keep?

## American Airlines failed greenmail, 1989 (operator 5)

Ch. 4.

Following successful greenmailing of Quaker Oats (1986, $30M+ profit) and Holiday Inn (1987, $30M+
profit), Trump bought a large stake in American Airlines' parent and threatened a hostile takeover.

American Airlines recognized the threat as bluster — Trump lacked the capital for an actual
takeover — and ignored him. Trump was forced to sell at a significant loss.

Trump declared: "I don't invest in the stock market. I don't trust it. Real estate is something I
can see and touch and control. Stocks go up and down, and you have no control."

**Decision effect:** the case is a data point for `TACO-pattern-recognition` (skill #2) — repeated
threats that can't be followed through on teach the market to call the bluff. For `overleverage`,
it's a signal that Trump's 1980s expansion had outrun his actual resources.

## The brand-licensing catalog, 2004–present (operator 6)

Ch. 4. Partial list of products/services bearing Trump's name, almost all pure licensing:

Trump Steaks, Trump Vodka, Trump Ice Natural Spring Water, Trump Signature Collection Menswear,
Trump Watches, Trump fragrances, Trump cologne, Trump home furnishings, Trump chocolates, Trump
magazine, Trump board game, Trump video game, Trump trading cards, Trump Mortgage, Trump Model
Management, Trump Productions, Trump sneakers, Trump coins, Trump guitars, Trump meme coins, Trump
tokens, Trump NFTs, Trump University, Trump Mobile (gold-painted Chinese phone), Trump Bibles.

Structure: Trump lends name, operator puts up capital, operator takes operational risk, Trump
takes royalty or equity regardless of operator outcomes.

**Decision effect:** if you are considering partnering with a famous brand in this structure,
model your economics with zero brand uplift and price the royalty as pure cost. If the deal
doesn't work on its own math, don't sign.

## Trump University, 2005–2016 (operators 1, 6)

Case cited by Sonnenfeld as exemplar of brand-licensing with customer fallout. Customers paid up
to $35K for "real estate courses" featuring a Trump cardboard cutout as the promised "photo with
Trump." Class-action lawsuits followed (settlement figure not cited in the chapters; omit if
referencing this skill as ground truth).

**Decision effect:** brand-licensed ventures where the brand-holder has no operational involvement
create a principal-agent gap: the operator captures fees, the customer bears disappointment, the
brand-holder captures royalty until reputational cost catches up. For anyone considering licensing
someone's brand into a customer-facing venture, the brand is not a substitute for operational
quality.

## West Side railyards / Television City, 1980s–1990s (operator 5)

Ch. 4. Trump bought an option on two derelict Penn Central railyards, envisioned a grandiose
"Television City" with skyscrapers, an on-site NBC studio, 3.6M sq ft of studio space, 8,000
apartments, 20,000 condos.

The deal required political connections of the kind Trump had with Beame. But Ed Koch succeeded
Beame, refused Trump's tax breaks, and kept NBC at Rockefeller Center with separate subsidies.

Trump stopped paying property taxes on the sites in the 1990s and surrendered control to a
foreign-investor syndicate for a meager sum — while negotiating to have his name placed on the
towers the syndicate eventually built. This created the public impression that Trump had built
the project. His name was stripped after 2016 when tenants voted to remove it for hurting resale
values.

**Decision effect:** Sonnenfeld's reading — with all things Trump, "even in successful projects,
often there is less substance than first appears." When a counterparty's brand is on a project,
verify the underlying operational and ownership facts; don't rely on the public impression.

# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 10 unless noted.

---

## Gold as brand identity (operators 2, 3)

Trump Tower on Fifth Avenue (completed 1983):
- Six-story pink-marble atrium with 80-foot waterfalls
- Brass-railed escalators "that gleamed like treasure"
- Gold-trimmed doors, gilded fixtures

Trump's personal apartment at the top of the tower: gilded molding, gold-leaf ceilings,
gold-plated fixtures.

Trump Taj Mahal, Atlantic City (opened 1990): marketed as "the eighth wonder of the world."
Crystal chandeliers. Gold-edged signage. Slot machines "designed to glitter."

Trump at the White House (2025): redecorated Oval Office in gilded style, gold trim across
once-sedate spaces. East Wing demolished for a "grand ballroom." Oval Office curtains
redesigned in gold tones. Air Force One plane donated by Qatar has abundant gold trim.

**Decision effect:**
- Operator 2 (armor): the volume and persistence of gold display exceeds any functional need.
  It is signaling, and what it signals is insistent wealth-status regardless of context.
- Operator 3 (class-for-the-masses): "gold screams money to the masses." The aesthetic is
  deliberately legible — no insider knowledge of pedigree needed. Elite critique ("tacky,"
  "gauche") reinforces rather than weakens the appeal to the target audience.

For a critic: don't lead with aesthetic critique. For an ally trying to appeal to the same
audience: match the accessibility register, not the specific gold aesthetic.

## Decoy elevator buttons (operator 2)

In any Trump skyscraper, elevator panels have more floor buttons than actual floors. Extra
buttons don't correspond to any floor; they are decoys.

Decision effect: the armor is structural, not merely rhetorical. A building engineered to appear
taller than it is, at the cost of operational honesty, is the pattern at its clearest.

## John Barron persona (operator 2)

1980s–1990s: Trump would personally answer phones when reporters called the Trump Organization
for comment, using a thinly altered voice, identifying himself as a spokesman named "John
Barron," and praising Donald Trump in the third person. "Mr. Trump is a great success. Very
rich, very powerful."

The charade was transparent — the voice was barely altered — but Sonnenfeld's point stands:
"if no one else would flatter him sufficiently, he would invent and impersonate someone who
did."

**Decision effect:** operator 2 at its most literal. The armor needs constant reinforcement; if
the external world doesn't provide enough, the leader manufactures it. This is useful for
detection: leaders who seem to constantly manufacture their own praise are showing the pattern.

## Ballroom obsession and East Wing demolition (operator 4)

Trump personally oversaw the demolition of the White House East Wing and construction of a new
ballroom. Sonnenfeld: the project "mattered very much to him personally"; he had cold-called
Obama advisors about the ballroom concept years before becoming president.

The official rationale (practical: host grand affairs without tents on the lawn) is not false
but incomplete. The deeper motivation per Sonnenfeld: "leaving his physical imprint on the most
iconic of American sites."

Rose Garden: Melania had spent a year carefully redesigning it during the first term. In the
second term, Trump paved over it in a few hours.

**Decision effect:** operator 4 predicts that a grandiose leader's energy and capital goes into
physical monuments, not programs. For a critic: focus not on "don't pave the Rose Garden" but on
"here is what those resources could have funded instead" — document opportunity cost.

## Trump-class battleships and Arc de Trump (operator 4)

December 2025: announcement of a new fleet of guided-missile warships, "Trump-class battleships."
Proposed "Arc de Trump" monument in DC, echoing the Arc de Triomphe.

Kennedy Center: Trump put his name on it. Ticket sales plummeted. Artists cancelled
performances. The reputational damage to the Kennedy Center was immediate; the name stayed.

**Decision effect:** operator 4 + operator 2 (armor). Even when naming-moves damage the
underlying institution, they serve the self-image purpose. This predicts that institutional
damage will not be a brake on self-naming decisions.

## Fragility under Jimmy Kimmel / late-night satire (operator 5)

Ch. 10: "This fragility helps explain Trump's hostility to satire (think Jimmy Kimmel), to
criticism (the press), to oversight (Congress)."

**Decision effect:** light mockery lands. The angrier and more disproportionate the leader's
response to a joke, the clearer the armor is thin at that specific point.

Practical example of the counter-move: a well-crafted SNL parody, a single viral joke that
captures a specific insecurity, a cartoon. These penetrate where 20-page policy critiques
don't. The counter-move of operator 5 is the most actionable piece of this entire skill for
most users.

## Susie Wiles's "alcoholic's personality" diagnostic (operator 2)

Chief of Staff Susie Wiles, quoted by Chris Whipple in *Vanity Fair*: Trump has "an alcoholic's
personality. He operates [with] a view that there's nothing he can't do. Nothing, zero,
nothing."

**Decision effect:** the armor pattern is visible to highest-level insiders. This is useful
evidence: the pattern isn't only observable from outside. Wiles is not an adversary; she is the
chief of staff. The testimony is not partisan critique; it is functional description.

For a user trying to calibrate: if your counterparty's closest aides privately describe the
leader in these terms, the armor reading is the correct reading.

## Inauguration crowd sizes (operator 2)

Trump's first inauguration: crowd was demonstrably smaller than Obama's 2009 and even Trump's
own 2025 event. Trump insisted it was the biggest ever; his then-spokesman Sean Spicer was
deployed to maintain the claim publicly.

**Decision effect:** operator 2 in its canonical form. Numbers are inflated even when the
unflated version would be politically adequate. The inflation is not tactical — it is
structural, it is reflexive, it is armor.

For a critic: documenting the inflation with specifics ("the actual attendance was X; the
claim was Y") is ongoing narrative-warfare work. See skill #5's
`challenge-false-claims-with-equal-vigor`.

## Article II "right to do whatever I want" (operator 8)

Trump "frequently described Article II of the Constitution as giving him 'the right to do
whatever I want.'" Sonnenfeld: "This was not a slip of the tongue or mere braggadocio, but a
fundamental worldview."

Also: Trump "echoed Napoleon's declaration that 'he who saves his Country does not violate any
law.'"

Schlesinger's *Imperial Presidency* (1973) is the academic framework. The 2020 election
subversion attempt, musings about serving more than two terms, praise of lifelong leaders,
mid-decade gerrymandering, flippant interference with state and local electoral officials —
all are operational forms of the claim.

**Decision effect:** the claim is prediction. Leaders who make the claim will take the
operational actions that follow from it. For institutional defenders, the planning horizon
begins when the claim appears, not when the first specific override happens.

## Heroic-identity vs. heroic-mission exemplars (operator 7)

Ch. 10's contrast:

**Heroic-mission exemplars:**
- George Washington — voluntary surrender of command (1783) and voluntary refusal to serve a
  third term (1797). Joseph J. Ellis *His Excellency* documents the "truly exceptional character"
  of this surrender.
- Lincoln — "If I had another face, do you think I'd wear this one?" Self-deprecating humor,
  unpretentious lifestyle, iconic log-cabin humility.
- Cincinnatus — Roman general who returned to his farm after saving the republic. Washington is
  likened to Cincinnatus in Garry Wills's *Cincinnatus: George Washington and the Enlightenment*.

**Heroic-identity pattern:**
- Alexander the Great — self-invented title, claim of descent from Odysseus and Achilles.
- Napoleon — the "saves his Country does not violate any law" rhetoric.
- Ozymandias (Shelley's 1818 poem) — the cautionary case.

**Decision effect:** the comparison can be deployed. When a grandiose leader invokes Lincoln or
Washington, the response should be specific: "Washington voluntarily surrendered command in
1783. Do you plan to do the same?" Comparisons with heroic-mission exemplars using the leader's
own register work where abstract critique does not.

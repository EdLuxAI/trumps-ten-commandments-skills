# Source Notes

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Chapter 10** ("Donald the Great"), with
supplementary material from Ch. 7 (class-for-the-masses) and Conclusion.

## Source caveats

`is_primary_source: false`. Sonnenfeld offers a Freudian reading of Trump's psychology (difficult
father, outer-borough insecurity, precarious tycoon credentials) that is interpretive, not
documentary. The operators describe the pattern; they do not depend on the specific biographical
claims being accurate in every detail.

Political loading: Ch. 10 is among the most partisan in the book, because grandiosity shades
into institutional-erosion concerns that Sonnenfeld treats as normatively alarming. The operators
are calibrated to be applicable regardless of whether the reader shares the normative concern —
but users should notice that the specific examples (Trump-class battleships, Rose Garden, East
Wing) carry the book's editorial voice.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `messianic-framing-I-alone-can-fix-it`

- **Joseph Campbell**, *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (Bollingen Series, Princeton UP, 1949).
  The "monomyth" — the hero's journey across cultures, centuries, civilizations. Campbell's
  framework identifies the "savior" archetype and its universal features. Sonnenfeld's claim:
  Trump's rhetoric taps this archetype intentionally (or instinctively).
- The specific phrase "I alone can fix it" is from Trump's July 2016 RNC convention speech.

### `grandeur-as-armor-for-insecurity`

- **Sigmund Freud**, psychoanalytic concept of defense mechanisms (*The Ego and the Id*, 1923).
  The grandiosity-as-compensation-for-insecurity pattern is a well-documented mechanism.
- **Alfred Adler** (early 20th c.), *The Neurotic Constitution* (1912). Adler's "superiority
  complex" — overcompensation for a persistent inferiority feeling — is the closest classical
  match for the pattern.
- **Susie Wiles**, Trump's chief of staff (2024–2025), quoted in Chris Whipple, *Vanity Fair*:
  Trump has "an alcoholic's personality. He operates [with] a view that there's nothing he
  can't do." Insider testimony, not a formal diagnosis.
- Sonnenfeld's specific reading of origin: domineering father Fred Trump, outer-borough
  Queens-not-Manhattan insecurity, smaller operational scale than real-estate peers (Sam Zell,
  Steve Schwarzman, Ed DeBartolo Jr., Stephen Ross, Donald Bren, Larry Silverstein), never ran
  a Fortune 500 company or answered to an independent board.

### `class-for-the-masses`

- **Thorstein Veblen**, *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899). Predicted (contra Marx) that
  Americans would emulate rather than resent success. Self-help literature tradition (Franklin's
  *Poor Richard's Almanack* 1732; Dale Carnegie *How to Win Friends and Influence People* 1936;
  Norman Vincent Peale *The Power of Positive Thinking* 1952; Stephen Covey *7 Habits* 1989) as
  the cultural matrix.
- **Horatio Alger** (1832–1899) — the rags-to-riches myth that "speaks to the wellsprings of
  American motivation," in Sonnenfeld's framing. The real Alger was a failed and disgraced
  preacher; the mythology was a later media construction.

### `physical-monuments-as-legacy`

- **Percy Bysshe Shelley**, "Ozymandias" (1818). Sonnenfeld's book coda. The cautionary text
  that grandeur is ephemeral.
- **Alexander the Great** — self-invented title claiming descent from Odysseus and Achilles.
  Sonnenfeld's reference point for the chutzpah required to manufacture mythic stature.
- **Napoleon** — "he who saves his Country does not violate any law" (attributed). Trump echoed
  this in defending his own authority claims.
- **FDR 1940 "Draft Roosevelt" convention** — meticulously choreographed theatrics, prearranged
  chants, strategically placed loudspeakers. Cited by Sonnenfeld as a historical precedent for
  presidential stagecraft, foundation for **Theodore H. White**'s *Making of the President*
  series (1961 onward).

### `fragility-under-mockery`

- No single formal source. Related concept: **narcissistic injury** in psychoanalytic tradition
  — the specific wound of being seen in a way that contradicts the maintained self-image.
- The book's specific observation: hostility to Jimmy Kimmel, to the press, to congressional
  oversight, to satire broadly. "Preserving that fragile illusion of greatness, no matter at
  what cost, becomes the only real, overarching leadership priority."

### `prefer-bilateral-to-multilateral`

- No formal academic source. The pattern is well-documented in international relations
  literature (e.g., **Kenneth Waltz**'s work on structural realism for the bilateral-vs-multi-
  lateral distinction at a systemic level). Here the operator describes an individual-leader
  preference.

### `heroic-identity-vs-heroic-mission`

- **Jeffrey Sonnenfeld**, *The Hero's Farewell: What Happens When CEOs Retire* (Oxford University
  Press, 1988). The distinction is Sonnenfeld's own. Heroic mission: quest for immortality
  through lasting contributions. Heroic identity: quest for stature and image of elevated,
  unique power. A CEO (or political leader) pathology is when heroic-identity dominates
  heroic-mission — the leader will refuse to leave office, will protect image over accomplishment,
  will undermine succession.

### `article-II-unbounded-authority`

- **Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr.**, *The Imperial Presidency* (1973). The post-WWII swelling of
  the presidency beyond constitutional design; the office accumulating "symbols and prerogatives
  of an imperial court" — ritual, courtiers, executive majesty. Sonnenfeld's framework for how
  far Trump's claims are from framer intent.
- **Trump's own statements**: Article II "gives me the right to do whatever I want"; Napoleon
  echo; public characterization of courts/intelligence/DOJ as illegitimate when ruling against
  him.
- **Related reading** Sonnenfeld cites: Tim Snyder, Anne Applebaum, Jason Stanley (political
  scientists on authoritarianism); Laurence Tribe, Judge Michael Luttig (legal scholars). These
  are contested politically but represent the mainstream legal-academy framing Sonnenfeld
  endorses.

## Fame / stage / Leo Braudy

- **Leo Braudy**, *The Frenzy of Renown: Fame and Its History* (Oxford UP, 1986). Fame breeds
  more fame; renown is insatiable. Once on the stage, you cannot step off without vanishing.
  Provides the framework for why grandiose leaders cannot de-escalate their own stage presence
  without existential threat to identity.

## Poetry

- **The Kinks**, *"Celluloid Heroes"* (1972) — "You can see all the stars as you walk down
  Hollywood Boulevard / Some that you recognize, some that you've hardly even heard of…" Used
  in Ch. 10 as meditation on fame's ephemerality.

# Source Notes

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Chapter 3** ("Divide and Conquer: Build
Walls, Not Bridges"). Material from the Conclusion on defender-side operators.

## Source caveats

`is_primary_source: false` — Sonnenfeld, as he discloses, was personally involved in catalyzing
several of the collective-response events cited as cases (the 2017 CEO Advisory Council
resignations, the 2020 Business Leaders for National Unity statement, the 2025 university
presidents' joint letters). The operators in this skill generalize across contexts; the specific
CEO-convening moves Sonnenfeld used are one template for coalition formation, not the only one.

Political loading: the chapter's framing of Trump as "the divider" and institutions as victims is
partisan. The divide-and-conquer pattern itself is non-partisan — it has been deployed by
attackers of many ideologies against coalitions of many ideologies. Operators presented here are
neutral to the attacker's or coalition's political direction.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `carrot-and-stick-prisoners-dilemma`

- **Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher**, RAND Corporation (1950). Formalized the prisoner's
  dilemma in game theory.
- **Robert Axelrod**, *The Evolution of Cooperation* (1984). Showed how repeated prisoner's
  dilemmas favor "tit-for-tat" cooperation — but also showed that once defection spreads, it is
  hard to reverse. This is why early collective commitment matters more than late.

### `collective-action-as-only-counter`

- **Benjamin Franklin** (attributed), July 4, 1776, on the signing of the Declaration of
  Independence: "We must all hang together, or assuredly we shall all hang separately." Quoted in
  the book by Amy Gutmann.
- **Mancur Olson**, *The Logic of Collective Action* (1965). Showed why large groups have
  systematic difficulty organizing around shared interests — the free-rider problem. The
  counter-moves in this skill (public pre-commitment, making defection visible) are specifically
  designed to overcome Olsonian collective-action failure.

### `target-the-weakest-member-first`

- No formal academic lineage. The pattern is intuitive in military strategy (hit the weakest
  flank first) and visible in the 2025 law-firm attack sequence.

### `wedge-issue-selection`

- No single academic source. Related concept: **coalitional instability** in political science
  (e.g., William Riker's *The Theory of Political Coalitions*, 1962) — minimum-winning coalitions
  are structurally unstable and vulnerable to wedge offers.

### `alliance-attacks`

- **Kurt Lewin** (MIT, 1940s), field theory and group dynamics. Foundational work on how
  in-group/out-group boundaries form, stabilize, and dissolve under external pressure. Lewin's
  insight: external pressure on a group can either unify it (if the group has strong internal
  bonds and a clear identity) or shatter it (if internal bonds are weak). This predicts which
  alliances will survive attack and which won't.

### `enlarge-the-pie-as-constructive-divide-and-conquer`

- **Herbert Kelman** (Harvard, 1970s onward), interactive problem-solving in international
  conflict. Kelman's method of "enlarging the scope of the problem" — forcing parties to imagine
  a desirable post-conflict future before negotiating current concessions — is the academic
  foundation for the Abraham Accords framing.
- **Jonathan Swift**, *Gulliver's Travels* (1726). Sonnenfeld's literary frame for how a strong
  individual perceives coalition resistance.

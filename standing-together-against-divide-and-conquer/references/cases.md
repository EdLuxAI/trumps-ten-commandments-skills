# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 3 unless noted.

## Paul, Weiss vs. Jenner & Block / WilmerHale / Perkins Coie, 2025 (operators 1, 3)

The Trump administration issued executive orders against major law firms that had investigated the
first Trump administration or employed attorneys who had. Paul, Weiss was targeted in March 2025.

Sonnenfeld's claim (disclosed as based on inside information): the real impetus was retaliation
for Paul, Weiss chairman Brad Karp's earlier campaign to rally support for peer firms facing
similar orders — i.e., for trying to build a coalition.

With the legal community splintered and no coordinated response, Karp faced partnership economics
he could not ignore: client flight risk is high and immediate for Big Law. He met with Trump for
three hours and settled. One of Trump's advisors on the call was the chair of Sullivan & Cromwell
— a Paul, Weiss rival. Other firms circled to poach senior partners and clients. A rival firm's
chairman inadvertently copied Karp on a 4 a.m. email outlining exactly that scheme.

**Three firms fought** — Jenner & Block, WilmerHale, Perkins Coie — and won restraining orders in
court. Hundreds of mid-sized firms eventually joined them, but only months later.

**Decision effect:**
- Operator 1: the prisoner's dilemma worked. Paul Weiss's partnership economics made it rational
  to settle individually, even though collective resistance would have served all firms better.
- Operator 3: Paul Weiss was targeted not because it was most culpable but because it had led
  peer defense — making it vulnerable as the coalition's organizer. Taking out the organizer
  prevents the coalition.
- Counter-counterfactual: if 20 firms had issued a binding joint statement *before* any firm was
  hit, the attack surface would have been the whole legal profession, and the pressure would
  have had to be political-national rather than firm-specific.

## Harvard, Columbia, and the Ivy League, Spring 2025 (operators 1, 2, 3, 4)

Trump administration cancelled $400M in federal funding to Columbia in spring 2025. Then-President
Katrina Armstrong accepted a series of demands, then resigned after campus backlash.

Emboldened, Trump froze $2.2B of federal research funds to Harvard with an even more sweeping set
of demands (an April 11 letter Sonnenfeld describes as "notoriously absurd"). Harvard President
Alan Garber, with full board backing (which included Ken Frazier, Ken Chenault, and Penny
Pritzker), refused.

Trump escalated: threatened tax-exempt status, cancelled billions in federal funding, moved to
strip ability to host foreign students. Simultaneously dangled favorable treatment for other Ivy
institutions (Dartmouth praised publicly) to drive wedges.

Unlike the law-firm community, the university response held together. Sonnenfeld helped convene
100+ current and former university presidents to publish collective letters in *Fortune*. Amy
Gutmann cited Franklin.

**Decision effect:**
- Columbia's early capitulation did not buy it lasting peace — it only emboldened the next
  attack.
- Harvard's refusal held because the board pre-committed to backing Garber and because 100+
  peers made the resistance visible.
- Operator 4 (wedge-issue-selection) failed here — Dartmouth did not accept the bait, and
  university presidents refused to let "institutional neutrality" fracture the common front.

## 2017 CEO Presidential Advisory Council walkout (operator 2)

After Trump's August 2017 response to the Charlottesville white-nationalist rally was widely seen
as insufficient, Ken Frazier (CEO of Merck) resigned from Trump's business advisory councils.
Trump attacked Frazier and Merck personally.

Within hours, CEOs of UPS, Walmart, Intel, PepsiCo, GM, IBM, BlackRock, and others walked out in
solidarity. The business advisory groups collapsed.

Sonnenfeld's framing (he was directly involved in convening the CEOs): "the first time in US
history that the US business community collectively rejected a US president's call to service."

**Decision effect:** the stampede happened in hours, not days. Once one credible CEO moved
publicly, others could follow. The speed of the cascade was the coalition. A slower response
would have given the administration time to pick off individuals.

## 2020 Business Leaders for National Unity statement (operator 2)

After Trump contested the 2020 election results, Sonnenfeld (at the request of several dozen CEOs
calling him directly) convened 94 renowned industrial leaders in twelve hours.

The group issued a statement with six specific bullet points:
1. Pride in encouraging the largest voter participation in US history (paid time off to vote)
2. Congratulating Biden and Harris as winners
3. Calling on Trump and anyone disputing the results to take grievances to court
4. Demanding that complaints reveal systematic fraud evidence
5. Acknowledging they saw no such evidence
6. Suggesting a speedy, transparent transfer of power

Released midday after Pennsylvania was called. Business Roundtable, US Chamber of Commerce, NAM,
and world leaders echoed the points within 24 hours.

Three more impromptu sessions convened: January 5 (before the Capitol attack), a few days after
January 6, and in April 2021 (voter-access laws).

**Decision effect:** the specific architecture of the statement — written, signed, numbered
bullets, 12-hour turnaround — is replicable. Coalition statements that are vague, unsigned, or
slow do not move subsequent events. Coalition statements that are specific, signed, and fast do.

## Mexico vs. Canada tariff treatment, March 2025 (operators 1, 4)

On the same day (March 6), Trump posted on Truth Social:

- **About Mexican President Sheinbaum:** "I have agreed that Mexico will not be required to pay
  tariffs on anything that falls under the USMCA Agreement… out of respect for, President
  Sheinbaum. Our relationship has been a very good one…"
- **About Canadian PM Trudeau:** "Believe it or not, despite the terrible job he's done for
  Canada, I think that Justin Trudeau is using the Tariff problem, which he has largely caused…"

Classic divide-and-conquer: reward one partner, punish the other, signal to future partners that
individual relationships with Trump yield individual outcomes.

**Decision effect:** multilateral allies who would benefit from presenting a unified position
(USMCA as a bloc, NATO as a bloc) are structurally disadvantaged if individual members accept
bilateral favors. The counter is to refuse selective favors publicly — which Canada and Mexico
did not do effectively here.

## AT&T / Time Warner / CNN / Jeff Zucker, 2017–2019 (operator 5)

AT&T tried to buy Time Warner (which owned CNN) in an $85B transaction. Pressure built on
AT&T CEO Randall Stephenson and Time Warner CEO Jeff Bewkes to fire Jeff Zucker and make CNN
coverage more Fox-like. They refused.

The DOJ Antitrust Division sued to block the merger on what Sonnenfeld describes as "frivolous
legal grounds" — despite the division chief Makan Delrahim having publicly supported the merger
months earlier ("I don't see this as a major antitrust problem").

The merger ultimately prevailed in court after 18 months of appeals. Sonnenfeld cites the
top leadership of both companies as blaming the delay for "epic failure" of the combined entity
— thousands of jobs lost, hundreds of billions in shareholder wealth destroyed.

**Decision effect:** the antitrust apparatus was the alliance-attack instrument. When regulatory
and institutional machinery is weaponized against specific coalitions (here, two companies
trying to protect editorial independence), the cost of resistance is paid even in victory. This
is why operator 5 emphasizes defending institutions *before* they are captured, not after.

## The Abraham Accords, 2018–2020 (operator 6)

Jared Kushner's work, building on the 2018 Bahrain Peace to Prosperity Summit. Sonnenfeld worked
closely with Kushner on the summit design.

The move: reverse the sequence of the previous 50 years of Mideast peace efforts (Oslo, Paris,
Annapolis) by starting with economic vision before political negotiation.

Kushner unveiled a $50B plan: grants + low-interest loans + private investment, projected to
double the Palestinian economy, create 1M jobs, reduce unemployment from 30% to single digits,
reduce poverty by 50%. US business leaders (Stephen Schwarzman, Randall Stephenson) attended to
endorse. Mohamed Alabbar (Emaar Properties, UAE): "By generating jobs, income opportunities and
filling gaps in delivering basic services, the private sector can help build momentum behind a
fragile economy."

Structurally, this used divide-and-conquer *constructively*:
- Isolated Iran, Hamas, Hezbollah as the "bad actors" with no seat at the prosperity table.
- Offered Arab Gulf states and Palestinians a visible, resource-backed alternative.
- Forced Gulf leaders to pick a side publicly — resulting in unprecedented Gulf-state
  vilification of Hamas and Hezbollah alongside diplomatic recognition of Israel.

**Decision effect:** the constructive inversion works only with credible resources and a
specific vision. Vague calls for unity do not produce these results; a $50B plan backed by
specific institutional commitments did. The "do-not-use-when" boundary (you need actual
resources) is not hypothetical.

# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 7 unless noted.

---

## Sarah Palin (operator 1)

Ch. 7. Palin was a strong early Trump ally in 2016 when few GOP establishment figures backed him.
She did nothing to cross Trump personally. But her star faded in the years that followed.

When the 2024 RNC convened, Palin could not get credentials to attend — as the only living
Republican VP-or-president nominee who had supported Trump. She complained publicly: "I didn't
even get credentials to attend the GOP convention, if you can believe it. I mean, wow, they're
tight."

**Decision effect:** operator 1 at its clearest. Loss of constituency alone was enough for
discard. No disloyalty, no falling-out. The operative variable was constituency, not loyalty.

## Rudy Giuliani, Michael Cohen, Mike Lindell, Sidney Powell (operators 1, 3)

Ch. 7. Trump allies who took on personal risk on his behalf and were discarded when their
reputations were sullied:
- **Giuliani** carried out "dirty hatchet jobs" (Sonnenfeld's framing) on Zelenskyy and the 2020
  election. Trump allegedly refused to pay his legal bills and froze him out. Partial
  rehabilitation via a pardon came only after repeated entreaties.
- **Cohen** allegedly acted on Trump's behalf in the Stormy Daniels hush-money payments, then
  went to prison alone.
- **Lindell** spent a significant chunk of his personal fortune promoting Trump's 2020
  election-denial claims. Substantively bankrupt, lawsuits pending. Trump did nothing to help.
- **Powell** was the "linchpin" of Trump's stolen-election lawsuits and was also discarded.

All four were "invented allies" (operator 3) — their public positions came largely through Trump
— and all four lost constituency once their roles became disreputable.

**Decision effect:** the combination of operators 1 and 3 produces the discard. Invented allies
with sullied reputations lose both their constituency value and their independent-power-base
protection.

## JD Vance, Marco Rubio, Lindsey Graham, Ted Cruz (operator 2)

Ch. 7. Former sharp critics rehabilitated once they retained constituency and bent the knee:

| Person | Prior criticism | Rehabilitation outcome |
|---|---|---|
| JD Vance | Called Trump "America's Hitler," "noxious," "heroin" | VP |
| Marco Rubio | "Lil Marco"; questioned Trump's manhood | Secretary of State + NSA |
| Lindsey Graham | "A choice between getting shot or taking poison" | Fierce defender |
| Ted Cruz | "Lyin' Ted"; Trump accused Cruz's father of JFK assassination | Trusted Senate ally |

Sonnenfeld's reading: the prior severity of criticism *added* to Trump's incentive to
rehabilitate. Converting a loud critic is a pure show of strength. Nothing signals dominance
like "my loudest critic now serves me."

**Decision effect:** operator 2. For anyone who is a former critic of a dominance-seeking
leader: your prior criticism may be leverage, not liability. For anyone who is a long-time loyal
supporter: your position is weaker than you think relative to newly-converted former critics.

## Elon Musk and Bobby Kennedy (operator 3, + operator 4)

Ch. 7. Sovereign allies with independent power bases — given wider berth despite independence
and even despite hostility.

**Elon Musk:** "Too powerful to be tossed aside, yet too independent to be embraced as an
unquestioningly loyal follower." Trump treats him "like a rival warlord — dancing awkwardly
around him, sometimes gingerly extending olive branches, waiting for the power dynamic to shift
and/or an opportunity to pounce, as if Musk is the American Prigozhin." Even after Musk called
for Trump's impeachment and surfaced Trump's appearances in the Epstein files, the two embraced
after the Charlie Kirk murder, with Musk invited back to events like the
Mohammed bin Salman state dinner.

**Bobby Kennedy:** "Trump clearly respects Bobby Kennedy's independent constituency and has
given RFK much more room to run and freedom to maneuver than his other cabinet secretaries."
Contrasted with Tulsi Gabbard. Trump quipped per *NYT*: "Bobby's a star. Tulsi? Tulsi wants to
be a star."

**Decision effect:** operator 3 predicts treatment by testing sovereignty. An advisor with
independent credibility (Musk's financial and technological standing; Kennedy's family
constituency) gets wider latitude. An advisor dependent on the leader gets no latitude.

## Mike Pence, January 6, 2021 (operator 1, Karl's *Retribution*)

Ch. 7. Pence served four years as VP, publicly loyal throughout. On January 6, 2021, he refused
to block the Electoral College certification.

Trump turned on him immediately; the Capitol crowd chanted "Hang Mike Pence."

Sonnenfeld's reading (via Jonathan Karl's *Retribution*): Pence "was genuinely startled and
caught off guard by how quickly Trump turned against him." But in the operator framework: by
2021, Pence had served his function of providing mainstream-conservative evangelical credibility
to the 2016 ticket. That constituency no longer required Pence specifically. He became "yet
another interchangeable, constituency-less official who could easily be tossed aside."

**Decision effect:** the combination of operators 1 and 5. Pence's defining moment was a single
procedural act of independence, interpreted as betrayal. With his underlying constituency
absorbed by Trump's own coalition, he was a cheap target.

## Marvin Roffman, 1990 (operator 8)

Ch. 7. The Roffman case is the canonical illustration of operator 8
(`preemptive-retaliation-via-asymmetric-leverage`).

Roffman was a research analyst at Janney Montgomery. He published research (correctly) that
the Trump Taj Mahal would be unable to generate enough revenue to cover its debts.

Trump's response: faxed the chairman of Janney Montgomery demanding Roffman be fired and a
public apology issued, threatening suit.

The firm folded: terminated Roffman.

Later, after the Taj Mahal bankruptcy confirmed Roffman's prediction, Roffman won a
multimillion-dollar wrongful-termination settlement. But the immediate goal (silencing the
critic) had been achieved.

**Decision effect:** Trump did not attack Roffman directly — he attacked Roffman's employer.
The asymmetric leverage (Trump's potential business with Janney >> Roffman's revenue to Janney)
made Roffman disposable by the firm. For anyone whose analytical criticism of a powerful figure
exposes them via an employer: build direct audience reach (today: Substack, independent research
outlets, direct subscriber models) so the employer is not the pressure point.

## Habba and Halligan US Attorney appointments (operator 6)

Ch. 7. 2025 appointments testing how far the retribution-via-prosecution pattern could go.

**Alina Habba** as interim US Attorney in New Jersey. The appointment "functioned as a
calculated test of institutional elasticity." It collided with:
- Statutory limits on interim appointments
- Judicial refusal to extend her tenure
- Unanimous appellate ruling stripping her of lawful authority

**Lindsey Halligan** in the Eastern District of Virginia. High-profile cases unraveled on:
- A judge finding her appointment unlawful
- A grand jury declining to indict
- Key evidence being barred

Trump's response to both pushbacks: retreat. "Trump ultimately largely let his efforts to pursue
legal retaliation lapse, abandoning his appointments of loyalists as US attorneys and reverting
back to the traditional blue-slip-Senate confirmation process."

**Decision effect:** operator 6 in action. The system pushed back, the leader pulled back. For
institutional defenders, this is encouraging: the boundaries can hold. But pullback is not
surrender — the leader will probe a different boundary next.

## Fed Chair Powell / Governor Lisa Cook (operator 7)

Ch. 7. Classic adjacent-weakness case.

Trump had been feuding with Fed Chair Jay Powell for years over interest rates. When Trump
attacked Powell directly — claiming the Fed had mismanaged construction projects with "billions
in taxpayer dollars" wasted — GOP senators rushed to Powell's defense. Direct attack on Powell
failed.

Trump pivoted: attacked Fed Governor Lisa Cook on mortgage-fraud allegations surfaced by
housing-agency head Bill Pulte (Cook had allegedly taken out multiple mortgages on different
properties each claimed as primary residence). Even Cook's supporters acknowledged the facts
looked "damning."

**Decision effect:** operator 7. The primary target (Powell) was too strong; the adjacent target
(Cook) was weaker. Cook may or may not be guilty of the specific allegations, but the attack
vector was chosen for leverage, not for substance. Pressure on Powell was indirectly
achieved via doubt cast on the Fed's leadership overall.

## Roger Stone, Paul Manafort, Michael Flynn, Steve Bannon (operator 2, martyr variant)

Ch. 7. Disreputable allies retained in the tent as martyrs:
- **Roger Stone** — sentence commuted; framed as "unfairly targeted by Mueller."
- **Paul Manafort** — rebranded from "for-hire felon" to "victim of the deep-state witch hunt."
- **Michael Flynn** — treated as "persecuted victim" of enemies.
- **Steve Bannon** — same. Even after being fired from the 2017 administration, retained as a
  loyalty figurehead.

Sonnenfeld: "These men are losers by conventional political standards. But because Trump can
spin their plights as symbols of a larger narrative of persecution and because he would rather
have them with him than against him, they are redeemed."

**Decision effect:** operator 2 in its constituency-as-symbolic-value form. Their constituency
is not a conventional audience — it is their symbolic role in the leader's persecution
narrative. Defending them defends the leader.

## The "buy low" on sympathetic losers (operator 1 + operator 2 hybrid)

Ch. 7. Trump's defense of disgraced figures who retain cult status:

- **Martha Stewart** (post-prison) — "remains glamorous and beloved and whose indictment aligns
  perfectly with Trump's sense of grievance over unfair treatment by prosecutors."
- **Mike Tyson** — "retains his die-hard fandom even though allegations of abuse have sullied
  his name."
- **Eric Adams** — "may be embroiled in tawdry scandals repulsive to Park Avenue but retains
  strong support within his working-class base in the NYC outer boroughs."
- **The Tate brothers** — "sex crimes shock polite society but they retain cult status on the
  extreme far-right."
- **George Santos** — commutation.

**Decision effect:** the operator's edge case. Trump's selective defense of these figures is not
loyalty and not moral judgment — it is "buy low" logic. A defending sympathetic-to-some figure
costs little (polite scorn from critics) while accessing their constituency and reinforcing the
leader's "persecuted-are-targeted" narrative.

## Jeff Sessions primary loss (operator 1, operator 5)

Ch. 7, Ch. 1. Sessions was Attorney General, then recused himself from the Russia
investigation. After leaving the administration, Sessions ran for his old Alabama Senate seat
(2020).

Trump endorsed Tommy Tuberville and attacked Sessions with enough force that Sessions
finished last in the primary, losing by double digits.

**Decision effect:** the recusal was a single procedural act. Sessions had no countervailing
constituency — his Alabama base had absorbed Trump — so retaliation was cheap. Operator 5
predicted the attack; operator 1 predicted its success.

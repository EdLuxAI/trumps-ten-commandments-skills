# Source Notes

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Chapter 7** ("Trump's World of Winners
and Losers: Class for the Masses, Avoiding Losers like the Plague, and Selective Retribution").
Supplementary material from Ch. 5 (`drown-the-squeaky-wheel-in-oil`) and Ch. 1
(`loyalty-is-one-way`, which is the exit-decision form of operators 1 and 5 here).

## Source caveats

`is_primary_source: false`. The book's specific characterizations of people as "losers" (Sarah
Palin, Reince Priebus, Sean Spicer, Jeff Sessions) carry Sonnenfeld's editorial voice. The
operators do not depend on agreeing with those characterizations — what matters is the *pattern*
of how the leader sorts, not whether any specific person deserves the sort.

Politically loaded framings to hold lightly:
- The "creeping authoritarianism" characterization of Trump's retribution pattern. The operator
  framework (selective retribution, push-until-system-pushes-back) is neutral to how the reader
  interprets the normative implications.
- The Sonnenfeld-catalyzed CEO counter-response activities (2017, 2020) he cites. Sonnenfeld's
  involvement is disclosed; the operator framework generalizes across leaders deploying the
  pattern.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `winner-vs-loser-by-constituency-not-virtue`

- **Niccolò Machiavelli**, *The Prince* (1513). The foundational separation of political
  effectiveness from personal virtue. Sonnenfeld does not cite Machiavelli directly, but the
  operator's logic (who retains support, not who deserves support, is the relevant variable)
  is direct descendant of Machiavellian realism.
- **Groucho Marx** (attribution): "I don't want to belong to any club that would have me as a
  member." Sonnenfeld uses this as the operative logic: "If the only people still loyal to you
  are nobodies, you are radioactive."

### `rehabilitate-if-constituency-remains`

- No formal academic source. The pattern is visible in the historical record of political
  leaders who converted prominent enemies (Lincoln's team of rivals — though Sonnenfeld
  contrasts this with Trump's pattern in Ch. 1). The conversion-trophy logic is Sonnenfeld's
  specific observation.

### `drop-invented-allies-keep-sovereign-ones`

- Related historical parallel: **Albrecht von Wallenstein** (Thirty Years War) — the
  independent-power-base problem in the Habsburg court. Too powerful to embrace, too
  independent to easily discard; eventually assassinated.
- **Yevgeny Prigozhin** (Wagner Group, Russia) — Sonnenfeld's explicit parallel in Ch. 7 for
  Elon Musk's status: "a threat to his own power who cannot be trusted and who has already
  crossed the Rubicon, and thus needs to be neutralized at the right time, but not
  immediately." Prigozhin's 2023 aircraft death after the Wagner mutiny is the contemporary
  reference point for the operator's downstream predictions.
- The logic connects to the broader Realpolitik tradition — **Clausewitz** *On War* (1832),
  **Otto von Bismarck**, **Deng Xiaoping**, **Lee Kuan Yew**, **George H. W. Bush**, **George
  Kennan**, **Henry Kissinger**, **Zbigniew Brzezinski** — all cited in Ch. 5 as pragmatic
  practitioners of relationship-based-on-strategic-interest.

### `drown-the-squeaky-wheel-in-oil`

- No formal academic source. The English expression "more flies with honey than with vinegar"
  is the cultural ancestor. The operator's specific move (absorption-via-attention rather than
  bribery-via-material-inducement) is Sonnenfeld's observation.

### `selective-retribution`

- No formal academic source. Related concept: **deterrence theory** in international relations
  (Thomas Schelling, *The Strategy of Conflict*, 1960). Selective retribution is
  deterrence-calibrated — attack only where the attack's cost/credibility ratio is favorable.
- **Jonathan Karl**, *Retribution* (2024). Cited in Ch. 7 for the Pence interview.

### `push-retribution-until-system-pushes-back`

- No single academic source. Related concepts:
  - **Institutional resilience** literature (various; e.g., Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson,
    *Why Nations Fail*, 2012) — institutions' capacity to absorb and repel pressure.
  - **"Stretch, not shatter"** — the operator's own framing. The leader probes rather than
    commits, which means institutional pushback actually works when properly deployed.

### `strike-adjacent-weakness`

- No formal academic source. Related concept: **flank attack** in military strategy (Sun Tzu;
  Clausewitz) — when the main line is too strong, attack the flank.

### `preemptive-retaliation-via-asymmetric-leverage`

- No formal academic source. The Marvin Roffman case (1990) is the book's exemplar. Related
  general concept: **chilling effect** in free-speech jurisprudence — when an attack creates
  a structural disincentive for future critics.

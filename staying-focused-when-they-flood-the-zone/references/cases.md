# Cases

All cases from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025), Ch. 6 unless noted.

---

## Cohen congressional testimony + Kim Jong Un summit, February 2019 (operators 1, 4)

Michael Cohen's February 2019 congressional testimony revealed (among other things) the Stormy
Daniels hush-money payments that would eventually convict Trump as a felon in New York.

The timing was not coincidental: Trump had scheduled a high-profile Kim Jong Un summit for the
same week. Many observers (including aides like John Bolton) feared Trump would trade major
concessions for empty promises just to have a diplomatic success to displace Cohen.

What actually happened: when Kim proved intractable, Trump did the unexpected — he walked away.
"Sometimes you have to walk, and this was just one of those times." Live TV flipped between
Cohen testimony and Trump's hastily scheduled press conference. On any other day, Cohen's
testimony would have been nonstop on every network.

**Decision effect:** a case of managed chaos (operator 4). Trump controlled the cadence, chose
what to amplify, and even the "failure" (walking from Kim summit) served a diversionary purpose.
For a defender, the lesson is: the story you think is dominant (Cohen's testimony) gets
displaced by a well-timed counter-event, even a counter-event that is itself a failure.

## Ukraine transcript voluntary release, 2019 (operator 2)

After a whistleblower (Col. Alexander Vindman) surfaced concerns about Trump's Zelenskyy call
(hundreds of millions in Ukraine military assistance and requests to investigate Biden), Trump did something
counterintuitive: he *voluntarily released* the verbatim transcript.

Many observers called this a blunder, assuming Trump thought the transcript would be
exculpatory. Sonnenfeld's reading: Trump knew exactly what the transcript contained, knew
Democrats would run with it into impeachment proceedings, and wanted precisely that — because an
impeachment battle would rally his base around an "us-vs-them" frame and displace the ongoing
2019 bad-news cycle. Historical backing for the move: public sentiment had rallied around Bill
Clinton during his impeachment despite the Lewinsky scandal.

**Decision effect:** operator 2. When an opponent voluntarily releases damaging material, the
question is not "do they realize this is damaging?" — they usually do. The question is "what
worse story is this displacing?" Keeping focus on the original bad-news cycle is the counter.

## The Epstein cascade, second term (operators 1, 2 backfire, 4, 5)

A rare case of real chaos rather than managed chaos.

Trigger: Pam Bondi's DOJ released a memo claiming there was no Epstein "client list" and
affirming the suicide ruling. Longtime loyalist FBI Deputy Director Dan Bongino was reportedly
ready to resign. Elon Musk posted that Trump appeared in the Epstein files.

Trump's initial tried-and-true response (it's an "us-vs-them" Democrat hoax, nobody cares
anymore) failed. Within days the *WSJ* broke the Epstein 50th-birthday letter (with a
hand-drawn voluptuous-woman figure).

Trump then tried operator 2: voluntary release of the Maxwell grand jury transcripts (after
sending his personal-lawyer-turned-Deputy-AG Todd Blanche to interview Maxwell). A judge
dismissed the motion — the transcripts would add nothing.

Then a rapid succession of diversion attempts:
- Sued *WSJ* for $10B
- Accused Obama of treason
- Threatened Rosie O'Donnell's citizenship
- Melania's $1B cease-and-desist to Hunter Biden
- Announced Coca-Cola's return to real cane sugar
- Called for Intel CEO's ouster
- Suggested Goldman's CEO "return to deejaying"
- Paved over the Rose Garden
- Took over DC police
- Deployed National Guard to LA, Memphis, Portland
- Plotted a Putin summit in Alaska

None worked.

Eventually Trump tried operator "invert-invert-invert": claim to have always been for Epstein
transparency, shifting attention to Larry Summers and Bill Clinton — which *did* work.

**Decision effect:**
- Operator 4: this sequence is the canonical case of real chaos. Compare the scattershot cadence
  and incoherent messaging to the Cohen/Kim case above.
- For a defender: during real-chaos periods, press the substantive story. The opponent's
  diversion capacity is temporarily exhausted. But prepare for managed-chaos resumption — real
  chaos is usually brief.

## "covfefe," May 2017 (operator 5)

Trump tweeted at midnight: "Despite the constant negative press covfefe" and left it up for
hours.

Public decoding: Was it a typo? Code? A medical episode? Speculation consumed news cycles for
days. #Covfefe trended. Late-night comedians ran with it. White House was asked about it
repeatedly.

What it displaced: Trump had fired FBI Director James Comey that same month, triggering the
Mueller special-counsel appointment — a news cycle that threatened to engulf the new
presidency.

**Decision effect:** sometimes the most effective distraction is the most inexplicable. A
typo that would have been nothing in most administrations became a week of coverage and gave
Trump narrative relief during a genuinely bad period. The counter is refusal to decode and
proportion of response.

## Access Hollywood + Bill Clinton accusers press event, October 2016 (operator 3)

The "grab 'em by the p*ssy" tape leaked. It would have ended most political careers.

Trump's response: held a surprise press event featuring four women who accused Bill Clinton of
sexual misconduct — Paula Jones, Juanita Broaddrick, Kathleen Willey, Kathy Shelton.

Broaddrick's line: "Trump may have said bad words, but Bill Clinton raped me and Hillary
Clinton threatened me."

Framing shifted from Trump's misogyny to partisan sexual allegations. Trump's base rallied.

Trump more or less dropped the four women after the election.

**Decision effect:** operator 3 in its most sophisticated form. The deflection didn't deny —
it counterattacked with framing that consumed comparable attention from the same audience.
Counter for the Clinton side would have required not taking the frame; they did not have the
tools to prevent it.

## Greenland / Canada-as-51st-state musings (operator 6)

Ch. 6 briefly: Trump has floated proposals that serve a moment's attention needs and then
disappears from them entirely. Greenland annexation. Canada becoming the 51st state.

What happened: the cycle that needed them was filled, the next cycle needed something else,
they disappeared.

**Decision effect:** operator 6's cadence. The foil doesn't need follow-through; it just needs
to fill the slot for a few days. Engaging the proposal substantively misallocates attention.

## Venezuela Maduro raid, 2025 (operator 1)

Announcement of a US military raid on Venezuela to seize Maduro for alleged drug trafficking
(and, per the book, "to secure opportunity for massive investments by US oil companies") wiped
24/7 coverage of Trump's domestic polling plunge.

Oil executives told Sonnenfeld they resented being used as cover — they had no advance
knowledge, and the oil glut made the investment premise implausible.

**Decision effect:** operator 1 at its cleanest. A major unrelated announcement at the exact
moment of peak unfavorable domestic coverage. For a defender, the temptation is to debate the
substance of the raid. The better move is to keep the original unfavorable story alive and
treat the raid on its own merits at its own cadence.

## DOGE cost-cutting promise → fizzle (operator 1)

DOGE (Department of Government Efficiency) was announced with Elon Musk and promised $2 trillion
in cuts. Actual cuts: ~$100 million, if that.

When it fizzled, Trump more or less washed his hands and blamed Musk. Moved on.

**Decision effect:** operator 1's dropping-accountability side. Ideas that don't pan out are
forgotten amid the next maelstrom. For a defender, documenting what was promised vs. delivered
is a small-but-real counter — you can't force accountability, but you can prevent the memory
from evaporating.

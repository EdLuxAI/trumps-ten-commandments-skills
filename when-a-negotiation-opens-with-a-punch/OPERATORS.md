## Per-Operator Notes

### 1. `open-with-maximalist-shock`
The organizing operator. Every other operator in this skill is either a variant (2, 3, 4) or a
response pattern (5, 6, 7, 8) that co-occurs with shock-opening. Decision effect: prepares the user
for the first 60 seconds of the meeting, which is where most negotiations are lost.

### 2. `wear-helmet-and-know-your-walk-away`
Pure defender operator. Placed second because it's what the user *does*, as opposed to what they
*recognize*. The framing "wear a helmet" comes directly from Sonnenfeld's Conclusion.

### 3. `leverage-by-adjacent-assets`
Structural move that happens before the negotiation. Once you're in the room, this has already been
played — the counter is anticipatory (map alternatives before committing). Mar-a-Lago and Winery
cases make the pattern memorable.

### 4. `declare-deal-before-deal-exists`
Timing-sensitive. The counter (same-day public clarification) is narrow and loses effectiveness
fast. High decision delta when it fires.

### 5. `disproportionate-escalation-when-challenged`
The Cohn playbook. Labeled `anti_pattern` from the user's perspective (recognize, don't mirror).
Cross-references lawfare material in skill #5 (narrative warfare).

### 6. `TACO-pattern-recognition`
A pure discount operator — learns to trust or distrust threats over time. Takes repeated encounters
to fire. Boundary: do not apply on first encounter.

### 7. `artificial-urgency-deadline`
Easily confused with real operational deadlines. The "ask what specifically happens at that
deadline" counter-move is cheap to deploy and cheap to reverse if the answer is substantive.

### 8. `post-deal-implementation-neglect`
The only forward-looking operator — addresses what happens *after* the negotiation. Included
because the book repeatedly shows that the real loss comes after signing, not during signing.

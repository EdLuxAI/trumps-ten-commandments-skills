# Source Notes

## Primary source

**Sonnenfeld, Jeffrey, and Steven Tian. *Trump's Ten Commandments*, 2025.** Primarily Chapter 2
("The Real Art of Trump's Deal"), with material from Chapter 4 (Deutsche Bank case) and the
Conclusion (defender-side framing).

## Source caveats

`is_primary_source: false` — Sonnenfeld interprets Trump's negotiation style from outside. The
operators generalize to any shock-opening counterparty and are not tied to the specific person.

Trump cases are 1970s–2025; the specific examples will age, but the pattern (maximalist open,
adjacent-asset leverage, manufactured momentum, disproportionate escalation) is older than Trump
and will outlast him.

## Academic and historical lineage per operator

### `open-with-maximalist-shock`

- **Roger Fisher & William Ury**, *Getting to Yes: Negotiating Agreement Without Giving In* (Harvard
  Negotiation Project, 1981). The foundational principled-negotiation text: separate people from
  problems, focus on interests not positions, invent options for mutual gain, use objective
  criteria. Sonnenfeld frames shock-opening as the diametric opposite.
- **Gerald Steinberg**, *Naval War College Review* critique of Fisher-Ury — argued it describes how
  the world should be, not how it is. Operators in this skill address the gap.

### `wear-helmet-and-know-your-walk-away`

- **BATNA (Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement)** — Fisher-Ury's defensive concept.
  "Wear-helmet" is Sonnenfeld's more vivid framing of the same discipline.

### `leverage-by-adjacent-assets`

- No named academic source. Pattern drawn from Trump's real estate career, especially the Mar-a-Lago
  (1985) and Trump Winery (2011) acquisitions.

### `declare-deal-before-deal-exists`

- No named academic source. Related concept in diplomatic literature: "fait accompli" diplomacy,
  where a party acts first and negotiates from the new status quo. Bismarck's unification of
  Germany is a classic case.

### `disproportionate-escalation-when-challenged`

- **Roy Cohn** (1927–1986) — New York lawyer, McCarthy-era prosecutor, later Trump's mentor and
  fixer. Cohn's maxim as Sonnenfeld cites it: "deny everything, fight back, and go on the offensive
  to declare victory."
- **George Washington**, letter to Major General John Trumbull (June 25, 1799): "offensive
  operations, often times, is the surest, if not the only (in some cases) means of defence."

### `TACO-pattern-recognition`

- **CNBC reporter Megan Cassella** — reported the acronym "Trump Always Chickens Out" that emerged
  from Wall Street trading desks during the April–May 2025 tariff flip-flops. Sonnenfeld documents
  150 tariff reversals in the first hundred days of Trump's second term.

### `artificial-urgency-deadline`

- No named academic source. Related concept: **scarcity principle** in Robert Cialdini's *Influence:
  The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984). Artificial deadlines are a manufactured form of the
  scarcity trigger.

### `post-deal-implementation-neglect`

- No named academic source. The book frames this as Trump's personal pattern (loves the chase,
  bored by implementation), but the general observation — that contracts require enforcement
  mechanisms, not just signatures — is a staple of commercial law and economic theory (see Oliver
  Williamson, transaction cost economics, 1975 onward).

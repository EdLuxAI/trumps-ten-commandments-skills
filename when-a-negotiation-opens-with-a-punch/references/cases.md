# Cases

All cases drawn from Sonnenfeld & Tian, *Trump's Ten Commandments* (2025) unless noted.

---

## Mar-a-Lago acquisition, 1985 (operator: `leverage-by-adjacent-assets`)

Ch. 2.

Marjorie Post bequeathed Mar-a-Lago to the US government as a winter White House. The Carter
administration refused the gift, citing maintenance costs. Post's descendants put the estate up for
sale.

Trump's opening offer: $15M. Rejected.

Trump then **bought the beach in front of Mar-a-Lago** and threatened to build a view-blocking
monstrosity. The descendants were now in a lose-lose: fight in court with little chance of winning,
or deal with the only bidder who had just engineered themselves into that position.

Final purchase: $5M for the estate + $3M for the antique furnishings. Today the property generates
$50M+/year.

**Decision effect:** if a counterparty has recently acquired something adjacent to your asset, your
apparent alternatives have probably already been eliminated. Map alternatives *before* the
negotiation; by the time the negotiation starts, the adjacency move has often already been made.

## Trump Winery (Kluge estate), 2011 (operator: `leverage-by-adjacent-assets`)

Ch. 2.

Patricia Kluge, ex-wife of former billionaire John Kluge, blew through her divorce settlement,
declared bankruptcy in the 2008 crisis. She asked Trump to help her sell her Charlottesville house
and vineyard for $100M.

Trump discovered that the adjacent land was held in trust for Kluge's son. He bought the **right
of first refusal** on that adjacent land for $500,000.

He then turned to Bank of America (which held the estate through foreclosure) and threatened to
build a twenty-foot concrete wall with "TRUMP" painted across it, directly in front of the house.

Final purchase: $13M. The estate came with $25M of wine in the basement as a free kicker.

**Decision effect:** same as Mar-a-Lago, one iteration on. The moves are repeatable. If you're
selling a distressed asset, assume a shock-opener will acquire adjacency before making you an
offer.

## Deutsche Bank, Trump International Chicago, ~2009 (operator:
`disproportionate-escalation-when-challenged`)

Ch. 2.

Deutsche Bank attempted to collect $40M that Trump had personally guaranteed on the Chicago tower
during the Great Recession.

Trump preemptively sued Deutsche Bank for **$3 billion** for "damage to his reputation" and
"undermining the project."

Deutsche Bank was forced into defensive position: countersuing for the original $40M while
defending the $3B claim and weathering PR damage during a period when they could ill afford it.

Outcome: despite having "essentially zero leverage from a financial perspective," Trump won a
loan-term extension through lawfare + PR pressure.

**Decision effect:** if you are trying to collect a debt from a counterparty with a lawfare
history, be prepared for the collection to cost you the institutional cost of litigation and
reputational damage. Build that into your calculus before you initiate collection.

## Doral / The Paint Spot, ~2014 (operator:
`disproportionate-escalation-when-challenged`)

Ch. 2.

During Trump's renovation of the Doral resort, Trump insisted on cheap Benjamin Moore Super Hide
paint. The paint peeled. The Trump Organization threatened Benjamin Moore with litigation and
media fallout if they didn't replace it; Benjamin Moore provided thousands of gallons of free paint
despite having done nothing wrong.

The local contractor quit. A small paint supplier, The Paint Spot, sued over an unpaid $32K bill.
Trump's lawyers fought the case through multiple procedural obstacles and appeals, to the point
where The Paint Spot owed $300K in legal bills over the original $32K.

**Decision effect:** disproportionate escalation is deployed even at tiny stakes, because the
deterrent effect on future small vendors is part of the point. If you're a small vendor in dispute
with a shock-opener, the rational calculation is often to escalate publicly (media, industry
networks) rather than relying on proportional legal remedies.

## Liberation Day tariffs, April 2, 2025 (operators: `open-with-maximalist-shock`,
`TACO-pattern-recognition`)

Ch. 2.

Trump announced a package of "reciprocal" tariffs including triple-digit rates on many countries,
with a 145% rate on China after retaliation.

Effects: stocks fell nearly 20% in five days; debt costs soared; global trade nearly halted.

Within 90 days: tariffs suspended, re-imposed, exempted-then-un-exempted across 150 separate
announcements. A universal 10% rate that would have been politically impossible to pass at
baseline became the "relief" after the triple-digit anchor.

Markets coined TACO — "Trump Always Chickens Out" — and began to discount tariff threats
automatically.

**Decision effect:** the Liberation Day case is the perfect paired case — it shows shock-opening
*succeeding* on the anchoring front (10% became palatable) and *failing* on the credibility front
(markets learned to discount). For a counterparty at the beginning of a relationship, take
threats seriously. For a counterparty with a track record, discount.

## Israel-Hamas hostage/ceasefire negotiations, October 2025 (operator:
`declare-deal-before-deal-exists`)

Ch. 2.

Trump publicly and repeatedly declared the Israel-Hamas war was over before any agreement existed.
He paired this with private pressure on all participants (Netanyahu, Egypt, Qatar, Turkey, UAE,
Morocco, Bahrain, Sudan) and floated extreme positions (US takeover of Gaza, Trump hotels) that
"lit a fire under the participants to forge a deal before Trump imposed an unfavorable deal on
them."

A Sharm el-Sheikh summit with 20+ Arab leaders followed. Trump signed individual declarations
with Egypt, Qatar, Turkey. A deal was reached — not the one Trump had announced, but close enough
that the announcement could be retroactively validated.

**Decision effect:** declaring-a-deal-before-deal-exists is a high-risk, high-reward move. It
works when participants fear looking like obstructionists more than they fear a bad deal. If you
are a participant, publicly clarify the actual state of negotiations quickly; otherwise the
announcement's anchor sticks.

## Racial discrimination suit (Cohn/Trump origin, early 1970s) (operator:
`disproportionate-escalation-when-challenged`)

Ch. 2.

The DOJ sued the Trump Organization for racial discrimination in housing (only a handful of Black
tenants across thousands of buildings). Most landlords would have settled for a nominal sum.

Cohn instructed Trump to fight — in court, in media, in politics — attacking the DOJ and claiming
unfair persecution. Ultimately Trump settled on terms similar to what had been offered from the
start, but the settlement did not admit guilt, allowing Trump to declare victory.

Cohn's playbook: "deny everything, fight back, and go on the offensive to declare victory." This
became Trump's lifelong template.

**Decision effect:** when you encounter a shock-opener with a genuine legal weakness, don't assume
the facts will speak for themselves. They will absorb the settlement cost and emerge with a
victory narrative. Plan your communication accordingly.
